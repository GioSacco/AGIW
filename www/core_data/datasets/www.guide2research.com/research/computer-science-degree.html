Computer Science Degree: Requirements, Salary and Career Prospects

  in Research   Posted on December 31, 2020

In today’s increasingly tech-driven world, having knowledge of computer science has never been more crucial. It not only allows individuals to thrive in the digital age; but education experts also recognize the relative importance of computer science as an underlying academic discipline for most fields of knowledge (Webb et al., 2017). As such, even K-12 students in the United States are now being taught basic computer science principles.

Beyond that, computer science has grown to become one of the most favored undergraduate programs of today. It is the third most popular degree among international students studying in the U.S. (InternationalStudent, n.d.). Furthermore, a study by Gallup that surveyed over 15,000 7th- to 12th-grade students and their parents in the U.S. revealed that 9 in ten parents want their child to pursue further studies in computer science upon graduating from high school (Gallup, 2014).

Before deciding if this degree is for you, however, it is important to learn more about the program. This article aims to shed some light on the topic by first discussing what is computer science. It also touches on the program’s requirements and costs as well as the available career opportunities for graduates. Ultimately, this will serve as a resource for students who have yet to find a program that suits them as well as students who want to know what to expect from this program so they can prepare themselves for it.

What is a computer science degree?

A computer science degree focuses on the study of the theoretical foundations of computation and information technology (IT) (Top Universities, n.d.). Its primary purpose is to shed light on the science behind computing technology and related applications.

Students enrolled in this program learn about the principles of computer science, the study of computational systems, and applications. They focus on theories, design, application, and development of software that make computational processes possible. Computation, in this context, refers to the form of calculation used in information processing. This calculation, in turn, utilizes well-defined models, such as protocols and algorithms (Top Universities, n.d.).

It is also good to note that computer science is a diverse discipline. Some may fall under the impression that it is just about programming codes. However, while programming is indeed a crucial element of computer science, it is just one of many. Computer science involves everything from the design to the analysis of algorithms and programs in order to ensure the efficient performance of both software and hardware.

Some of the sub-disciplines of computer science include:

  • Data systems and management
  • Networking and administration
  • Artificial intelligence
  • Game development
  • Operating systems
  • Software engineering
  • Computer graphics and animation
  • Website technologies
  • Database design and management
  • Cryptography and security
  • Scientific computing

The principles and methodologies of computer science are required in essentially every industry in today’s technologically-powered world. This is why an undergraduate degree in computer science is considered as one of the most stable and lucrative options for incoming college students.

What a computer science degree entails and what to expect

The ultimate purpose of a computer science degree is to teach and develop the knowledge and skills integral to computer science. Students are taught and trained on how to develop, test, and apply programs and processes related to computers and computational systems. 

A computer science degree is designed to produce future computer scientists who possess a deep understanding of the underlying principles behind computational design, programs, and algorithms. As such, this program equips students with basic and advanced information on computational systems, programming, and more. 

For these to be learned and accomplished, computer science degrees are often structured in an incremental manner. A typical undergraduate computer science degree takes four years to complete. During the first year, students will learn about the basic principles. In the following years, they will take units in more advanced classes, such as computational theories, network theories, pattern recognition, and machine learning, among other foundational theories. 

Times Higher Education

As a student progresses through the course, they will gain a mix of practical and specialist skills that will prepare them for the demands of the field. They will deal with more advanced modules, including but not limited to data structures, complexity theories, patterns, signals, and software engineering.

Towards the end of the course, students are required to demonstrate what they have learned in the previous semesters. The students’ knowledge and capabilities will be gauged through a final project that will challenge and highlight their skills at the same time. 

Many universities also match final year students with industry partners and computer science researchers. This gives students opportunities for professional training and internships, thus exposing them to real-world experiences.

Motivations for Taking Up Computer Science

Today’s modern world heavily relies on technology and connectivity, and this trend is not going away anytime soon. Rather, it will simply continue to evolve. Because of this, the demand for computer science experts is bound to remain. The abundance of opportunities after graduation is just one of the reasons why computer science is attractive among incoming college students. That aside, some of the other common motivations that encourage students to take this course are as follows:

1. Computer science degrees pave the way for better hiring prospects.

Computer science degree graduates are often welcomed to the workforce with better full-time employment prospects. In 2019, there were about 500,000 computer science-related job openings. Computer science jobs also recorded the second-highest average starting salary ($67,539), next only to engineering jobs (Sanderson, n.d.).

2. Computer science degrees set students up for recession-proof jobs.

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the computing and IT job sectors are projected to grow by up to 37% through 2022. There are more jobs that need to be filled than qualified applicants. Following this trend, it is easy to see that computer science graduates have a recession as the least of their concerns. After all, their jobs are paramount to driving industries and economies further.

3. Computer science degrees prepare students for the future.

Computer science jobs are the jobs of today and the future. Studying this course in the U.S. prepares students for the demands of the industry not just in the country, but internationally. Furthermore, when students already possess sufficient foundational knowledge and skills, they can easily continue learning to keep themselves up-to-date with emerging trends and industry developments.

Considerations When Applying for a Computer Science Degree

Given the reasons that make computer science degrees valuable and in-demand, it is not surprising that many incoming college students consider this course. However, as almost all undergraduate institutions in the U.S. offer computer science degree programs, students need to establish their own criteria for choosing a program and institution to attend. Those criteria should include the following:

1. The reputation of the institution and employability rate

Possibly the most important factor to consider when choosing an institution to attend college is its reputation, especially in the field of computer science. Students may check the number of awards and fellowships the institution has previously awarded.

It is also wise to research the employability rate of the school’s graduates. Knowing this gives students an idea of how well-regarded the institution is and its graduates across the industry. 

2. Cost of studying computer science

The total cost of studying a computer science degree in the U.S. varies greatly, depending on a number of factors. Aside from tuition fees, students also need to consider books, school supplies, boarding, food, travel, and other living expenses.

According to the average tuition fees published on StudyPortals, computer science degrees at most U.S. state and flagship universities can cost anywhere between $5,000 to $20,000 per year. Meanwhile, tuition fees for programs that offer specific concentrations, such as AI, robotics, gaming, and bioinformatics, typically start at $9,000.

3. Availability of financial assistance

An undergraduate institution’s reputation is more often than not, a huge factor in the cost of its programs. Students looking to attend prestigious schools may check out available funding and financial assistance to see whether they are eligible for some. 

In the U.S., there are several scholarship programs available for STEM programs, including computer science. The AFCEA STEM Majors Scholarships, for instance, are offered to undergraduate and graduate students majoring in STEM subjects. Another example is the Betty Stevens Frecknall Scholarship, which is a computer science scholarship awarded to full-time undergraduate STEM students residing in the U.S.

4. Concentration and specialization

For students looking to focus on a specific major, they should also find out if the institution they are eyeing offers that particular major. Otherwise, they may need to look for another school that better matches their program preferences.

5. Research opportunities

Students may also want to check whether a school has sufficient research opportunities and facilities in various areas of computer science. Attending an undergraduate institution that supports computer science research can help prepare students for graduate studies.

6. Lab facilities and technology

Computer science is a highly technical degree. Thus, an institution offering the course should have the facilities and equipment necessary for effective teaching and learning. Students should consider finding schools with dedicated computing laboratories that come with high-performance computers and servers. These should also be equipped with the proper programs, such as MATLAB and C++/Java, among others. 

Admission Requirements for a Computer Science Degree

Entrance requirements for undergraduate programs such as computer science are generally the same. Students are expected to present a high school diploma and pass the minimum aptitude test scores. Students may also need to establish their capacity to pay for the program. 

Diploma requirements

Colleges and universities offering computer science degrees require official high school transcripts. Students transferring from another school also need to present transcripts from their previous school.

Home-schooled students, on the other hand, can also present their diploma if their secondary schooling was monitored by an accredited school. Otherwise, they may provide a copy of their high school work portfolio. They may also present proof of their general education development (GED) credential. 

Meanwhile, students who already have an associate’s degree may be admitted into a four-year computer science degree program without presenting their high school diploma. The same goes for applicants with at least 60 college credits.

Aptitude test scores

Schools also consider students’ scores in their Scholastic Assessment Test (SAT) or American College Testing (ACT) examinations. The New York Institute of Technology, for instance, requires a minimum combined SAT score of 1080, including a minimum score of 550 in math. This is equivalent to an ACT score of 21.  However, college institutions weigh SAT and ACT scores differently. Some take them as a huge deciding factor. Meanwhile, other schools prioritize high school coursework and grade averages over SAT and ACT results. Different schools have different minimum score requirements as well.

High school level coursework requirements

College applicants are not usually expected to have previous formal training and education in computer science. However, those who have basic knowledge of at least one programming language and how computers work in general might be able to ease into the course faster. 

Computer science degree entry requirements also typically emphasize inclination in mathematics and science. A student should have passed three years of mathematics (algebra and geometry) and science (physics and laboratory science) at the high school level. Students are also expected to be proficient in English.

For example, The New York Institute of Technology requires incoming Computer Science, B.S. degree students who do not meet the minimum required SAT score to have a minimum cumulative GPA of 2.0. Applicants should have completed at least 12 credits of math, physics, computer science, and engineering, with a GPA of 2.3 for each course.

Aside from the general requirements listed above, different institutions may each have unique entrance requirements, such as personal essays, referrals, and interviews. Colleges may also have different deadlines for entrance application submissions.

What can you do with a computer science degree?

One of the questions that students and parents may have in mind is, what can you do with a computer science degree? Fortunately, this college degree opens the doors to a wide range of career opportunities. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projected that by 2020, there will be around 4.2 million new jobs in the computing and IT sectors in the U.S. (Montoya, 2017). As technology and connectivity continue to be integrated with practically every industry, it is safe to say that computer science degree holders are least threatened with unemployment. Many institutions report an average of 89% to 93% career-track job placement for their graduates, each getting at least two job offers at a time (InternationalStudent, n.d.).

The ongoing demand for computer scientists in the U.S. can be attributed to the lack of qualified candidates. Many prospective students are probably unaware of how good the job prospects are, in terms of both compensation and meaningful, satisfying work (Mehta & Uhlig, 2017).

However, students should know that career options for computer science degree graduates are not limited to just a few. Graduates can choose to apply for systems administrator jobs or focus on software programming. They can easily work their way up the career ladder.

Computer Science Certifications and Associations to Take Note Of

Computer science graduates can further improve their footing in the industry by acquiring certifications related to their chosen line of work or concentration. These certifications can help graduates stand out among the competition. Some of the computer science certifications available in the U.S. are:

  • Certified in Risk and Information Systems Control (ISACA) – This certification equips IT professionals with the skills and knowledge that prepare them for the risks and challenges unique to the industry.
  • Certified in Information Security Management (ISACA) – This certification recognizes a computer science professional’s knowledge of international security practices.
  • Citrix Certified Enterprise Engineer (Citrix) – This certification is given to experienced IT professionals who are adept at the design and integration of various Citrix systems.
  • Cisco Certified Network Association (Cisco) – Also known as the CCNA Routing and Switching certification, this establishes an IT professional’s knowledge of foundational technologies and skills relevant to the adoption of next-generation systems.
  • CompTIA Network+ (CompTIA) – This is a globally-recognized vendor-neutral networking certification that recognizes one’s skills in designing, configuring, managing, and troubleshooting wired and wireless networks.
  • CompTIA A+ Technician (CompTIA) – This is an entry-level certification that focuses on vendor-neutral PC technologies. It is recommended for computer technicians.

Furthermore, computer science graduates can also join professional associations. This provides opportunities for networking and improving one’s credentials. Some of the industry associations to join are:

  • Association for Computing Machinery
  • Computing Research Association
  • Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers

Choosing a Computer Science Degree and School

Innate talent and interest in computer science can take one to greater heights, and the journey can begin by choosing the right computer science degree program and school. Fortunately, the U.S. is a hub for undergraduate institutions that offer competitive programs. Students only need to take their time assessing their goals and looking at a school’s reputation, facilities, and learning opportunities offered to ensure that they obtain a fruitful college experience.
